\begin{abstract}
We note an algebraic equivalence: the radial acceleration relation (RAR),
when rearranged to isolate the dark-matter contribution, is identical to the
Bose-Einstein occupation number $\nbar(\varepsilon) = 1/(\exp\varepsilon - 1)$,
with dimensionless energy $\varepsilon = \sqrt{\gbar/\gdag}$ and
$\gdag = 1.2 \times 10^{-10}\,{\rm m\,s^{-2}}$.
This is not a fit but a rearrangement of the published RAR formula.

To test whether this scale preference is robust and not a numerical
coincidence, we develop a kernel+scale identifiability framework with
anti-numerology controls.  Applied to \ValNData\ SPARC rotation-curve
measurements, the Bose--Einstein RAR kernel is preferred over six
alternatives (including hyperbolic cotangent, logistic, and power-law
controls), localizing a preferred scale at
$\log_{10}(s/{\rm m\,s^{-2}}) = \ValBestLogScale$---within
\ValDeltaFromGdag~dex of $\gdag$.  Galaxy-grouped 5-fold
cross-validation gives
$\log_{10} s = \ValCvMean \pm \ValCvStd$.
Global permutation and within-galaxy circular shift nulls yield zero hits
in 500 realizations at both $\pm 0.10$ and $\pm 0.05$~dex windows
(Clopper--Pearson bounds $\leq \ValAOneCPUpperTen$).  Negative controls
confirm that the signal collapses under predictor noise, variable warping,
and galaxy-label randomization.  Extension to cluster-scale data does not
recover $\gdag$, serving as an explicit null.

Using this framework, we test whether the residual statistics simplify in
the mapped coordinates, using rotation-curve data spanning four quality
tiers: a core sample of 131 SPARC galaxies (2{,}777 points), an extended
Tier~K sample of 250 galaxies (9{,}406 points), and independent validation
datasets including PROBES (1{,}506 galaxies) and KiDS-1000 weak lensing.

Three statistical fingerprints emerge:
(1)~the variance of RAR residuals scales as $\nbar^2$
($\daic = +10.5$ vs.\ Poisson $\nbar$ scaling);
(2)~the scatter derivative changes sign within $0.06\dex$ of $\gdag$,
robust across four non-parametric methods and 25 binning configurations;
and (3)~a candidate excess kurtosis spike at $\gdag$
($\kappa_4 = 20.7$; 95\% CI $[5.1, 29.0]$), absent in a structure-only
semi-analytic null ($\kappa_4 \approx 0.01$), but dependent on B-band
mass models requiring independent confirmation.

We subject these fingerprints to a primary battery of 16 core and 7 supporting
statistical tests, plus 12 supplementary BEC diagnostics recovered from the
pipeline audit. In the primary battery, 10 support consistency with the
Bose-Einstein mapping, 5 are inconclusive or fragile under covariate
control, and 1---replication in the independent PROBES catalog---fails,
with the scatter inversion displaced by $1.2\dex$ from $\gdag$.
The supplementary diagnostics strengthen evidence for non-random residual
structure but also reveal confounds (distance quality, size scaling) that
must be controlled explicitly.
We present a full scorecard, discuss whether the features reflect
coincidence, a $\lcdm$ consequence, or wave dark matter, and identify
discriminating tests for forthcoming data.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction} \label{sec:intro}

The radial acceleration relation \citep[RAR;][]{McGaugh2016} is one of the
tightest empirical scaling relations in extragalactic astronomy. It connects
the observed centripetal acceleration $\gobs = V_{\rm obs}^2/R$ in disk
galaxies to the Newtonian acceleration generated by baryons alone, $\gbar$,
through
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rar}
    \gobs = \frac{\gbar}{1 - \exp\!\left(-\sqrt{\gbar/\gdag}\right)},
\end{equation}
where $\gdag = 1.2 \times 10^{-10}\,{\rm m\,s^{-2}}$ is the sole free
parameter \citep{Lelli2017}. Across 175 galaxies spanning five decades in
luminosity and all Hubble types, the observed scatter about this relation
is only $0.13\dex$ \citep{Li2018}---comparable to measurement uncertainties.

The RAR's tightness and universality have resisted easy explanation. Modified
Newtonian dynamics \citep[MOND;][]{Milgrom1983} postulates a modification
of gravity at low accelerations but does not predict the scatter structure.
Fuzzy dark matter \citep[FDM;][]{Schive2014,Ferreira2021} produces solitonic
cores but has not been connected to the RAR's functional form. Meanwhile,
$\lcdm$ simulations can reproduce the mean relation through baryonic feedback
\citep{Keller2017,Ludlow2017}, but the origin of its specific mathematical
form remains unexplained.

A natural question is whether $\gdag$ is merely a fitting parameter
or a physically meaningful scale that reappears in independent
contexts.  The answer has implications for dark matter models:
MOND predicts $\gdag$ as fundamental, while $\lcdm$ must explain
its emergence from galaxy formation physics \citep{Desmond2017,Navarro2017}.

In this paper, we report a simple mathematical observation:
Equation~(\ref{eq:rar}), written in terms of the dark matter contribution,
is algebraically identical to the Bose-Einstein occupation number. We then
develop a \emph{kernel+scale identifiability framework} with three categories
of anti-numerology controls to test whether this scale preference is robust:
(i)~scale injection scans measuring how sharply the AIC minimum
localizes the preferred scale;
(ii)~permutation-based null tests verifying that random pairings
do not reproduce the localization; and
(iii)~nearby-scale comparisons against cosmologically motivated
alternatives ($a_\Lambda$, $cH_0$, $cH_0/6$).
Finally, we catalog the statistical consequences of the identification
across multiple datasets spanning three orders of magnitude in sample size,
and subject the findings to a comprehensive battery of robustness and
replication tests.

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Scope of claim.} This paper reports:
(i)~an exact algebraic equivalence between the published RAR and the BE
occupation number;
(ii)~a validated kernel+scale framework demonstrating that the BE~RAR kernel
is preferred over six alternatives, with the preferred scale localized to
within \ValDeltaFromGdag~dex of $\gdag$;
(iii)~three residual-statistical features anchored at $\gdag$, tested against
a structure-only semi-analytic null model (not full-physics $\lcdm$);
(iv)~a 35-analysis audit (23 primary tests + 12 supplementary diagnostics)
with an honest scorecard including failures; and
(v)~falsifiable predictions for forthcoming data. We do not claim that
these features require a Bose-Einstein condensate interpretation.

\section{The Algebraic Identity} \label{sec:identity}

\subsection{Derivation}

Equation~(\ref{eq:rar}) can be rewritten by isolating the dark matter
contribution. Define $\gdm \equiv \gobs - \gbar$. Then:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gdm_ratio}
    \frac{\gdm}{\gbar} = \frac{\gobs}{\gbar} - 1
    = \frac{1}{1 - \exp\!\left(-\sqrt{\gbar/\gdag}\right)} - 1.
\end{equation}
Using the identity $1/(1-e^{-x}) - 1 = 1/(e^x - 1)$, this becomes
\begin{equation}\label{eq:be_identity}
    \boxed{\frac{\gdm}{\gbar}
    = \frac{1}{\exp\!\left(\sqrt{\gbar/\gdag}\right) - 1}
    \equiv \nbar(\varepsilon),}
\end{equation}
where $\varepsilon \equiv \sqrt{\gbar/\gdag}$ is a dimensionless energy
parameter and $\nbar(\varepsilon) = 1/(e^{\varepsilon} - 1)$ is the
Bose-Einstein occupation number for modes of energy $\varepsilon$ at unit
temperature.

This is not a fit: it is a rearrangement of the published RAR formula
(Figure~\ref{fig:identity}). The dark-matter-to-baryon acceleration ratio
\emph{is} the mean occupation number of a bosonic distribution, with the
baryonic acceleration playing the role of mode energy (through the
square-root dispersion relation $\varepsilon = \sqrt{g/\gdag}$) and $\gdag$
playing the role of the condensation temperature.

Three physical regimes emerge naturally:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Condensed} ($\gbar \ll \gdag$, $\nbar \gg 1$): the
    ``dark matter dominated'' outer regions of galaxies, where flat rotation
    curves indicate large occupation numbers.
    \item \textbf{Transition} ($\gbar \approx \gdag$, $\nbar \sim 1$): the
    boundary where MOND-like phenomenology appears.
    \item \textbf{Thermal} ($\gbar \gg \gdag$, $\nbar \to 0$): inner
    baryon-dominated regions where Newtonian dynamics hold.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Cosmological Connection} \label{sec:cosmo}

The acceleration scale $\gdag$ is numerically proximate to the de~Sitter
acceleration. \citet{Verlinde2017} derives $a_0 = cH_0/6$, which yields
$a_0 = 1.182 \times 10^{-10}\,{\rm m\,s^{-2}}$ for
$H_0 = 73\,\kms\,{\rm Mpc}^{-1}$ \citep{Riess2022}---a 1.5\% offset from
the observed $\gdag$ (varying from 1.5\% to 9\% across the current $H_0$
range). As an aside on functional-form fit quality, the
\citet{Milgrom1999} de~Sitter--Unruh interpolation function achieves a
lower AIC than Equation~(\ref{eq:rar}) by $\daic = -1{,}222$, with the
improvement concentrated in the dark-matter-dominated regime.

\begin{quote}
\textit{AIC methodology note.} This $\daic$ is a statement about
interpolation-function fit quality, not about the physical validity of
either framework. The comparison uses
${\rm AIC} = \chi^2 + 2k$ where
$\chi^2 = \sum_i [(\log g_{{\rm obs},i} - \log g_{{\rm model},i})/\sigma_i]^2$
with uniform per-point weights in $\log\gbar$ and $\sigma_i$ set to the
global RMS (see Appendix~\ref{app:aic}). Both models have $k=1$ free
parameter ($\gdag$). Under inverse-variance weighting, the preference
shrinks to $\daic \approx -400$; under galaxy-averaged weighting,
$\daic \approx -80$. In all schemes, the Milgrom form is preferred; only
the magnitude changes. This comparison is secondary to the paper's main
contribution---the kernel+scale identifiability framework---and is included
for completeness.
\end{quote}

These observations are consistent with $\gdag$ being set by the cosmological
constant $\Lambda$ rather than evolving with the Hubble parameter, as
independently suggested by the exclusion of $\gdag \propto cH(z)$ at
$\sim\!5\sigma$ from the baryonic Tully-Fisher relation to $z \approx 2.5$
\citep{Sanders2008}. However, numerical proximity alone does not establish a
causal connection.

\section{Kernel+Scale Identifiability Framework} \label{sec:methods}

To test whether the BE~RAR identification reflects a genuine scale
preference or a numerical coincidence, we develop a reusable
kernel+scale identifiability framework with built-in anti-numerology
controls.

\subsection{Kernel Library}\label{sec:kernels}

We define seven candidate kernel functions $K(x, s)$ mapping a
physical variable $x$ (acceleration) and a free scale parameter $s$
to a predicted response $y$:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{BE\_RAR} (Eq.~\ref{eq:rar}): $K = 1/(1 - e^{-\epsilon})$,
        $\epsilon = \sqrt{x/s}$.
  \item \textbf{BE\_occupation}: $K = 1/(e^{\epsilon} - 1)$.
  \item \textbf{BE\_cousin}: $K = \epsilon/(1 - e^{-\epsilon})$.
  \item \textbf{coth}: $K = \coth(\epsilon)$.
  \item \textbf{tanh}: $K = \tanh(\epsilon)$.
  \item \textbf{logistic}: $K = 1/(1 + e^{-(\epsilon-1)})$.
  \item \textbf{power\_law}: $K = \sqrt{x/s} + 1$ (no special scale).
\end{enumerate}

For the SPARC RAR, we use ``direct'' mode: $y = K(x, s)$ with zero
nuisance parameters, because the RAR mapping \emph{is} the kernel.
This gives the fairest comparison: all models have exactly one free
parameter (the scale $s$).

\subsection{Scale Optimization}\label{sec:scaleopt}

For each kernel, we perform a two-stage optimization:
(i)~grid search over $N_{\rm grid}$ log-uniformly spaced trial
scales in $[10^{-13}, 10^{-8}]$~m~s$^{-2}$, followed by
(ii)~bounded scalar minimization (Brent's method) seeded from the
best grid point.

We compute the Akaike Information Criterion
${\rm AIC} = 2k - 2\ln\hat{L}$, where $k$ is the number of free
parameters and $\hat{L}$ is the Gaussian likelihood evaluated at
the RMS residual.  The Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) is
computed in parallel.

\subsection{Scale Injection Scan}\label{sec:scalescan}

To characterize how sharply the data prefer a particular scale, we
evaluate the AIC across 300 trial scales spanning the full search
range.  From this curve we extract:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\daic_{\pm 0.1}$: the AIC increase when the scale is shifted
    $\pm 0.1$~dex from the optimum;
  \item $\daic_{\pm 0.1}/N$: the same, normalized by sample size
    (dataset-independent identifiability metric);
  \item \textbf{Peak sharpness}: the second derivative of the AIC
    with respect to the log-scale at the optimum,
    $\mathcal{S} \equiv d^2({\rm AIC})/d(\log_{10} s)^2 |_{s=\hat{s}}$,
    estimated by finite differences on the scan grid.  Larger values
    indicate a narrower, more tightly localized minimum.  Units are
    AIC per $(\log_{10}\,{\rm m\,s^{-2}})^2$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Null Tests and Structure-Preserving Control}\label{sec:nulls}

We employ three destructive permutation-based null tests and one
structure-preserving control:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Global shuffle} (A1): permute $y$ relative to $x$,
    destroying all correlation.
  \item \textbf{Block-permute bins null} (A2b): randomly reassign
    which bin's $y$-values go with which bin's $x$-values (0.5-dex
    bins).  This preserves the within-bin marginal distribution of
    $y$ but breaks the cross-bin monotonic relationship that encodes
    the characteristic scale.
  \item \textbf{Within-galaxy circular shift} (A3): for each galaxy,
    circularly shift its $y$ values by a random offset, preserving
    per-galaxy distributions but destroying the radial alignment.
  \item \textbf{Within-bin shuffle} (A2, control): permute $y$ only
    within coarse $x$-bins (0.5~dex width).  This preserves the
    conditional structure $p(y \mid x)$ approximately at the chosen
    bin width and is \emph{not} a null test: $p \approx 1$ is expected
    up to finite-binning and refit variability.
    It confirms that the scale preference resides in the global
    $x$--$y$ correlation, not in bin-local structure.
\end{enumerate}

For each null realization, we refit the best kernel and record the
optimal scale.  The test statistic is
$p_{\pm w} = N_{\rm hits,\pm w}/N_{\rm shuffles}$, where a ``hit''
is $|\log_{10} s_{\rm null} - \log_{10} \gdag| < w$ for
$w \in \{0.10,0.05\}$~dex.  When $N_{\rm hits,\pm w}=0$, we report
the one-sided 95\% Clopper--Pearson upper bound.

\subsection{Galaxy-Grouped Cross-Validation}\label{sec:groupcv}

To prevent data leakage (points from the same galaxy appearing in
both train and test), we use GroupKFold with $k=5$ folds where
group~$=$~galaxy.  For each fold we record the best kernel, optimal
scale, and out-of-sample RMSE.

\subsection{Nearby-Scale Comparison}\label{sec:nearby}

We compare $\gdag$ against cosmologically motivated scales:
$a_\Lambda = c^2\sqrt{\Lambda/3}$, $cH_0$, $cH_0/6$ (Verlinde),
$cH_0/(2\pi)$.  This is done in two modes:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{$\eta = 1$ (fixed)}: diagnostic direct substitution
    where the scale is fixed (no optimization) and the likelihood is
    evaluated directly with zero free parameters.
  \item \textbf{$\eta$ free}: fit $y = \eta \cdot K(x, s_{\rm named})$
    with one free parameter per model (matched degrees of freedom).
\end{enumerate}

AIC values from the $\eta=1$ and $\eta$-free modes are not directly
comparable because the parameter count differs; model-selection claims
are restricted to matched-DoF comparisons.

\subsection{Cluster-Scale Null}\label{sec:cluster_null}

Applied to the \citet{Tian2020} cluster RAR, the framework does

$a_0 \approx 14 \times \gdag$ \citep{Tian2020}, and our framework
correctly reports a null.

\section{Three Statistical Fingerprints} \label{sec:fingerprints}

If the identity in Equation~(\ref{eq:be_identity}) is more than a
mathematical curiosity---if it reflects an underlying wave or condensate
nature of dark matter---then the \emph{residuals} about the RAR should carry
specific statistical signatures. We test three such fingerprints using the
SPARC core sample and Tier~K extended data (Table~\ref{tab:tiers}).

\subsection{Variance Scaling} \label{sec:variance}

For a bosonic field, the variance of occupation number fluctuations is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:variance_models}
    \sigma^2 = \begin{cases}
        A\,\nbar(\nbar + 1) + C & \text{(quantum BEC)}, \\
        A\,\nbar^2 + C & \text{(classical wave)}, \\
        A\,\nbar + C & \text{(Poisson/particle)},
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $A$ captures the signal amplitude and $C$ the observational noise
floor \citep{Cheong2024}. We bin RAR residuals from the \textbf{SPARC core
sample} by $\log\gbar$ and compute the variance in each bin
(Figure~\ref{fig:variance}). Fitting the three models yields:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Wave ($\nbar^2$) vs.\ Poisson ($\nbar$): $\daic = +10.5$ in
    favor of wave scaling.
    \item Quantum ($\nbar^2 + \nbar$) vs.\ classical wave ($\nbar^2$):
    $\daic = -0.79$---\emph{indistinguishable}.
\end{itemize}

The data strongly prefer wave-interference scaling over Poisson, but the

\citep{Cheong2024}. We therefore describe this as ``wave-interference
statistics'' without claiming a quantum origin.

\subsection{Scatter Inversion at $\gdag$} \label{sec:inversion}

The standard deviation of RAR residuals, $\sigma(\log\gbar)$, is not
monotonic. Its derivative $d\sigma/d(\log\gbar)$ crosses zero---a scatter
``inversion''---at a location that coincides with $\gdag$.

Using four independent non-parametric methods (RAR parametric, LOESS, cubic
spline, isotonic regression) on the \textbf{SPARC core sample}, the
zero-crossing lies at $\log\gbar = -9.970 \pm 0.006$ (method-to-method
range), only $0.050\dex$ from $\log\gdag = -9.921$
(Figure~\ref{fig:inversion}). A parametric phase-diagram model locates a
variance peak at $\mu_{\rm peak} = -9.922$ ($0.0015\dex$ from $\gdag$),
with $\daic = -225$ relative to a monotonic baseline.

\subsection{Kurtosis Spike at $\gdag$} \label{sec:kurtosis}

\textbf{Systematics caveat (front-loaded):} The kurtosis result below
depends on the B-band mass models in the \citet{Korsaga2019} subsample
within Tier~K. SPARC-only data (3.6\,$\mu$m photometry) show Gaussian
residuals at $\gdag$ ($\kappa_4 = 0.64$). This feature is a \emph{candidate}
signal requiring confirmation with homogeneous near-infrared mass
decomposition. Readers should weight this fingerprint accordingly.

\medskip
A phase transition at $\gdag$ would produce non-Gaussian residuals localized
at the critical scale. We compute the excess kurtosis $\kappa_4$ of RAR
residuals in bins of $\log\gbar$ using the \textbf{Tier~K extended sample}
(250 galaxies, 9{,}406 points; Table~\ref{tab:tiers}).

\begin{itemize}

    \item In a structure-only semi-analytic null

    \S\ref{sec:lcdm_null}): $\kappa_4 = 0.01$ at $\gdag$.
    \item Within the Korsaga subsample alone: $\kappa_4 = 23.95$.
    \item Within SPARC-only data: $\kappa_4 = 0.64$ (Gaussian).
    \item The kurtosis derivative peaks at $\log\gbar = -9.939$, only
    $0.018\dex$ from $\gdag$.
\end{itemize}

Disambiguation analysis (\S\ref{sec:kurtosis_disambig}) rules out geometric
origin: the RAR slope maximum ($\log\gbar = -8.5$) and curvature maximum
($-9.38$) do not coincide with the kurtosis peak. The spike is driven by
the Korsaga B-band subsample, not by RAR curvature.

\section{Internal Robustness} \label{sec:robustness}

We subject the scatter inversion---the most robust of the three
fingerprints---to a battery of internal consistency tests. Results are
summarized in Table~\ref{tab:robustness}.

\subsection{Non-parametric Consistency}

Four independent smoothing methods (RAR parametric, LOESS, cubic spline,
isotonic regression) applied to the SPARC core sample all recover the
scatter inversion within $0.006\dex$ of each other
(Table~\ref{tab:robustness}). The inversion is not an artifact of any
particular smoothing choice.

\subsection{Binning Robustness}

Across 25 binning configurations (widths $0.20$--$0.70\dex$, 5 offsets per
width), all 25 recover a zero-crossing. The mean inversion is
$\log\gbar = -9.890 \pm 0.180$, with a mean distance of $0.128\dex$ from
$\gdag$. The best configurations (bin width $0.27$--$0.35\dex$) recover
$\gdag$ to within $0.002$--$0.016\dex$. Wider bins ($\geq 0.50\dex$)
degrade precision, with the worst case drifting to $0.55\dex$ from $\gdag$.

\subsection{Jackknife Stability} \label{sec:jackknife}

Leave-one-out jackknife over 131 SPARC galaxies yields an inversion-point
standard deviation of $0.005\dex$ and a maximum shift of $0.031\dex$. All
131 jackknife inversions fall within $0.20\dex$ of $\gdag$. Four galaxies
(IC~2574, NGC~2915, NGC~2976, UGC~07577) are influential for the
\emph{environmental scatter} sign, but none destabilize the inversion
location.

\subsection{Split-Half Replication}

In 1{,}000 random half-splits of the 131 SPARC galaxies: 95.7\% of halves
reproduce the inversion within $0.20\dex$ of the baseline, and both halves
simultaneously agree in 91.4\% of splits. Size-balanced splits yield 93.2\%
agreement. Random thirds: all three subsets within $0.20\dex$ in 73.1\% of
cases.

\subsection{Monte Carlo Distance Injection}

Injecting random distance errors of $5\%$--$30\%$ into 131 SPARC galaxies
(1{,}000 Monte Carlo iterations per level), the environmental scatter
difference remains stable up to $\sim\!15\%$ errors. Beyond $20\%$, the
confidence interval for the high-acceleration environmental delta widens to
include zero.

\subsection{Environmental Scatter Uniformity} \label{sec:env_scatter}

RAR scatter shows no significant difference between field (83 galaxies) and
dense (48 galaxies) environments in the SPARC core sample. Point-level
Levene $p = 0.64$; galaxy-level Levene $p = 0.76$; error-corrected Levene
$p = 0.45$; block-permutation ($N = 10{,}000$) $p = 0.74$.

However, this result is \emph{fragile under covariate control}. The field
and dense subsamples are imbalanced in distance ($p = 0.001$), inclination
($p < 0.001$), and gas fraction ($p = 0.043$). After propensity-score
matching (29 pairs), the low-acceleration scatter difference becomes
$+0.008\dex$ ($p = 0.12$)---not significant but with reduced power. The
Mahalanobis-matched subsample (96 galaxies) still recovers the inversion
within $0.08\dex$ of $\gdag$, but propensity-matched subsamples do not,
largely due to reduced sample sizes.

\subsection{Structure-Only Semi-Analytic Null} \label{sec:lcdm_null}

As a baseline comparison, we construct a structure-only $\lcdm$ null model:
500 synthetic galaxies with NFW halos drawn from the observed
stellar-mass--halo-mass relation \citep{Ludlow2017}, exponential baryonic
disks, and log-normal scatter in stellar mass ($0.15\dex$) and concentration
($0.11\dex$). This model \emph{intentionally excludes baryonic feedback},
serving as a structure-only null hypothesis---not as a test of $\lcdm$ in
its full-physics form.

The null model's scatter inversion falls at $\log\gbar = -11.6$, displaced
by $1.7\dex$ from $\gdag$. Only 5 of 100 Monte Carlo realizations produce
an inversion within $0.20\dex$ of $\gdag$. Full hydrodynamic simulations
(e.g., EAGLE, IllustrisTNG) with galaxy-adapted aperture radii have not
been tested with identical analysis pipelines and may produce different
results.

\section{External Validation and Replication} \label{sec:external}

\subsection{Extended RAR Tiers} \label{sec:extended}

Adding non-SPARC rotation curves in quality tiers, the inversion is
replicated when data quality is controlled (Table~\ref{tab:external}):
\begin{itemize}
    \item Tiers~A through B+ (SPARC + THINGS + de~Blok, up to 152
    galaxies): inversion shifts by $< 0.015\dex$.
    \item Tier~K (+ Korsaga, 250 galaxies): inversion at $-9.865$, shifted
    $0.29\dex$ from SPARC-only baseline.
    \item Full Tier~C (all sources, 374 galaxies): inversion at $-9.947$,
    shifted $0.21\dex$.
\end{itemize}

Crucially, the scatter quality degrades with non-SPARC sources:
SPARC $\sigma = 0.17\dex$, THINGS $\sigma = 0.13\dex$, but
Korsaga $\sigma = 0.33\dex$ and PHANGS $\sigma = 0.47\dex$.
The inversion is preserved when restricting to sources with
$\sigma < 0.2\dex$.

\subsection{PROBES: Replication Failure} \label{sec:probes}

The PROBES catalog \citep{Stone2022} provides an independent sample of
1{,}506 galaxies (45{,}176 points after excluding 112 SPARC overlaps).
The scatter inversion in PROBES falls at $\log\gbar = -11.12$, offset by
$1.20\dex$ from $\gdag$---\textbf{a clear failure to replicate}.

Across 8 binning configurations, the mean PROBES crossing is
$-11.21 \pm 0.06$, with none within $0.20\dex$ of $\gdag$. The PROBES RMS
scatter ($0.32\dex$) and mean offset ($0.44\dex$) are substantially larger
than SPARC, consistent with heterogeneous mass-model quality degrading the
signal. This failure is the most important negative result in our test
battery and constrains the physical interpretation: either the inversion
requires SPARC-quality mass decomposition ($\sigma < 0.2\dex$) to detect,
or it is a feature of the SPARC sample specifically.

\medskip

\medskip

\subsection{Weak Lensing Validation} \label{sec:lensing}

Using KiDS-1000 stacked lensing profiles \citep{Brouwer2021} in four
stellar-mass bins, the isolated-galaxy subsample shows consistently tighter
RAR scatter than the full sample, with the environment effect growing with
mass (field-dense $\Delta\sigma = 0.04$--$0.08\dex$). This pattern is
consistent with the BEC prediction that tidal interactions in dense
environments disrupt condensate coherence
(but see \S\ref{sec:not_established} for fragility caveats).

However, NFW profiles are preferred over cored (solitonic) profiles at all
mass bins ($\daic > 160$), because the predicted BEC core radius
($\xi \approx 2$--$10\,{\rm kpc}$) falls below the lensing inner radius
($\sim\!35\,{\rm kpc}$). Current stacked lensing cannot resolve scales
where BEC signatures are predicted to appear.

\subsection{ALFALFA $\times$ Yang BTFR} \label{sec:btfr}

Cross-matching ALFALFA $\alpha$.100 with \citet{Yang2007} group catalogs
yields 2{,}273 galaxies (1{,}917 field, 356 dense). The baryonic
Tully-Fisher scatter is \emph{larger} in dense environments at all velocity
widths, with the strongest effect at low $W_{50}$
($\Delta\sigma = 0.43\dex$, Levene $p = 0.02$).

This pattern does not match the simple BEC prediction of
environment-independent scatter at low accelerations. Whether it reflects
genuine BEC-inconsistency or systematic differences in the ALFALFA sample
(single-dish HI widths vs.\ resolved rotation curves) remains an open
question.

\subsection{Cluster Scale} \label{sec:clusters}

Applied to the \citet{Tian2020} CLASH cluster-scale RAR, the framework does
not recover $\gdag$: the cluster acceleration scale is
$a_0 \approx 17 \times \gdag$. No significant correlation between
per-cluster phase boundary and velocity dispersion is found
($B = 0.014 \pm 0.229$, $p = 0.95$). This is consistent with either a
mass-dependent BEC coherence length or distinct physics at cluster scales.

\subsection{Kurtosis Disambiguation} \label{sec:kurtosis_disambig}

The kurtosis spike does not coincide with the RAR slope maximum
($\log\gbar = -8.5$) or curvature maximum ($-9.38$), ruling out a
geometric artifact. However, it is driven primarily by the Korsaga B-band
subsample ($\kappa_4 = 23.95$), with SPARC contributing only
$\kappa_4 = 0.64$. Addition of 82 MHONGOOSE rotation-curve points leaves
the spike unchanged ($\kappa_4 = 20.7 \to 20.7$; bootstrap CI shift
$< 0.1$), indicating robustness to new data but not resolving the B-band
dependence.

\subsection{IllustrisTNG Healing-Length Comparison} \label{sec:tng_xi}

If the Bose-Einstein mapping is physical, variance in the residuals should
concentrate near the healing length $\xi = \sqrt{G M_{\rm dyn}/\gdag}\,/\,
{\rm kpc}$. A na\"{\i}ve comparison of 131 SPARC galaxies against 48{,}060
IllustrisTNG (TNG100) halos shows apparent organizing in TNG
($C = 1.55$, permutation $p < 0.001$) but not in SPARC
($C = 0.69$, $p = 0.56$), where $C$ is the ratio of variance at
$0.3 \leq R/\xi \leq 3$ to the full-profile variance. This discrepancy
is confounded: the two samples span different baryon-mass ranges.

We control for this using nearest-neighbor mass matching
($|\Delta\log M_{\rm bar}| \leq 0.3\dex$; 131 pairs). In the matched
sub-samples, the TNG stacked concentration collapses to $C = 0.13$
(95\% CI $[0.07, 0.50]$, permutation $p = 1.00$), while SPARC is
unchanged ($C = 0.69$, 95\% CI $[0.41, 1.01]$, $p = 0.56$). Neither
matched sample shows significant $\xi$-organizing. The full-sample TNG
signal was driven by sample composition, not a physical healing-length
structure.

A paired Wilcoxon signed-rank test on 110 galaxies with valid per-galaxy
$C$ detects a distributional asymmetry ($p = 9.9 \times 10^{-7}$), with
median $\log_{10}(C_{\rm TNG}/C_{\rm SPARC}) \approx 0.02$ but
mean $\approx 0.17$, indicating a right tail of high-$C$ TNG galaxies
rather than a systematic shift. At fixed baryon mass the TNG dynamical
masses are systematically larger (median $\log_{10}(M_{\rm dyn,TNG}/
M_{\rm dyn,SPARC}) = +0.21$), producing healing lengths $\sim\!28\%$
larger (median $\log_{10}(\xi_{\rm TNG}/\xi_{\rm SPARC}) = +0.11$).
This $M_{\rm dyn}$ offset is the physical lever behind the
$\xi$-distribution difference.

To verify that this offset is not an artifact of the outermost-point
kinematic $M_{\rm dyn}$ estimator, we perform two independent checks.
First, comparing $V_{\rm flat}$-based and outermost-point dynamical
masses for 57 SPARC galaxies yields median
$\log_{10}(M_{\rm dyn,Vflat}/M_{\rm dyn,outer}) = -0.013$
(std $= 0.019\dex$; 95\% CI $[-0.017, -0.002]$), confirming that the
kinematic proxy is internally stable to $< 0.02\dex$---an order of
magnitude smaller than the TNG offset.
Second, cross-matching ALFALFA $\alpha$.100 with Yang~DR7 group halo
masses (1{,}070 galaxies at fixed $M_{\rm bar}$) gives a Yang--kinematic
gap of $+1.77\dex$ (95\% CI $[1.46, 1.92]$), consistent with the
expected aperture mismatch between virial-scale $M_{200}$ and
rotation-curve-extent $M_{\rm dyn}$. The TNG--SPARC offset ($+0.21\dex$)
is only $\sim\!12\%$ of this full halo gap, indicating that TNG's excess
dynamical mass at fixed $M_{\rm bar}$ reflects inner halo
over-concentration rather than a systematic underestimate by the SPARC
kinematic proxy.

\subsection{What Is Established}

Three results are robust:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The RAR is algebraically identical to the Bose-Einstein
    distribution (Equation~\ref{eq:be_identity}). This is a mathematical
    fact.
    \item The BE\_RAR kernel is preferred over six alternatives in a
    head-to-head comparison with anti-numerology controls. The preferred
    scale is localized to within \ValDeltaFromGdag~dex of $\gdag$, with
    zero hits in 500-realization global and galaxy-shift permutation
    nulls (Clopper--Pearson bound $\leq \ValAOneCPUpperTen$), and
    galaxy-grouped 5-fold CV giving
    $\log_{10} s = \ValCvMean \pm \ValCvStd$.
    \item RAR residual variance follows wave-interference scaling
    ($\sigma^2 \propto \nbar^2$), not Poisson
    ($\sigma^2 \propto \nbar$), with $\daic = +10.5$.
    \item The scatter inversion is anchored at $\gdag$ with sub-$0.1\dex$
    precision in the SPARC core sample, reproduced across 4 non-parametric
    methods, 25 binning configurations, 1{,}000 split-half trials (91.4\%
    success), and 131 jackknife removals (std $= 0.005\dex$). It is absent
    in a structure-only semi-analytic null (offset by $1.7\dex$).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{What Is Not Established} \label{sec:not_established}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Quantum vs.\ classical.} The quantum prediction
    $\nbar(\nbar+1)$ is indistinguishable from the classical $\nbar^2$
    ($\daic = -0.79$). Approximately $4.75 \times 10^6$ data points are
    needed for a $3\sigma$ distinction.
    \item \textbf{$\lcdm$ exclusion.} Our structure-only null fails to
    reproduce the features, but full-physics hydrodynamic simulations have
    not been tested with identical pipelines. We explicitly do not claim
    that $\lcdm$ is excluded.
    \item \textbf{Kurtosis provenance.} The spike relies on Korsaga B-band
    mass models ($\kappa_4 = 23.95$); SPARC alone gives $\kappa_4 = 0.64$.
    \item \textbf{Independent replication.} The PROBES catalog does not
    reproduce the scatter inversion. Whether this reflects data quality
    ($\sigma_{\rm PROBES} = 0.32$ vs.\ $\sigma_{\rm SPARC} = 0.17$) or
    the absence of a physical signal cannot be determined without
    homogeneous mass decomposition.
    \item \textbf{Environmental scatter.} The null result is BEC-consistent
    but fragile: covariate imbalance (distance, inclination, gas fraction)
    between field and dense subsamples may mask a real difference.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Three Possibilities}

The observations are consistent with three interpretations:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item[(a)] \textbf{Coincidence}: the RAR's functional form happens to
    match the BE distribution, and the statistical features at $\gdag$ arise
    from the structure of SPARC galaxy mass profiles, disappearing in other
    catalogs (PROBES).
    \item[(b)] \textbf{$\lcdm$ consequence}: baryonic feedback produces RAR
    statistics with BE-like structure, and $\gdag = cH_0/6$ arises from the
    cosmic baryon fraction.
    \item[(c)] \textbf{Wave dark matter}: dark matter is a bosonic field
    whose collective dynamics produce BE occupation statistics, with $\gdag$
    as a physical condensation threshold set by $\Lambda$, detectable only
    with high-quality mass decomposition.
\end{enumerate}

Possibility (c) connects to the wave dark matter literature:
\citet{Cheong2024} derive $\nbar(\nbar+1)$ fluctuations from the bosonic
density matrix; \citet{Centers2021} confirm Rayleigh statistics in
virialized FDM simulations; \citet{Khoury2016} and \citet{Berezhiani2015}
predict condensation in galaxy halos from superfluid dark matter. At cluster
scales, \citet{Tian2020} find $a_0 = 1.73 \times 10^{-9}\,{\rm m\,s^{-2}}
= 14.4\times\gdag$, with a mass-dependent trend ($r = 0.58$, $p = 0.008$)
consistent with a healing length $\xi \propto M^{-0.27}$.

No current test uniquely selects among these three possibilities.

\subsection{Physical Interpretation} \label{sec:phys_interp}

This paper establishes that the Bose--Einstein kernel provides a
statistically preferred description of the RAR, with scale localization at
$\gdag$ surviving all null and robustness tests we can construct from the
SPARC data. These are statements about galaxy rotation curves and the
mathematical structure of the RAR; they do not require or assume any
particular model of compact objects, and the analysis is self-contained at
the galaxy scale.

For this framework to advance beyond a phenomenological identification,
future work must target galaxy-level discriminants that separate the
BEC interpretation from alternatives (c.f.\ the three possibilities above):
\begin{itemize}
  \item High-resolution samples (e.g.\ BIG-SPARC) enabling direct
    measurement of the healing-length parameter $\xi$ as a function of
    galaxy mass.
  \item Morphology-, stellar-mass-, and environment-matched comparisons
    that isolate the condensate signal from covariate confounds.
  \item Forward-modeling of BEC halo density profiles to predict rotation
    curves, rather than post-hoc fitting of the RAR residuals.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{How to Extend This Framework} \label{sec:extend}

To test whether $\gdag$ appears in a new dataset:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Define $(x, y)$ where $x$ has acceleration dimensions.
  \item Choose a fit mode (``direct'' if $y$ is a known function of
    $K(x,s)$; ``log'' or ``linear'' otherwise).
  \item Run the experiment with the same kernel library and null tests.
  \item A ``second appearance'' detection requires:
    $\daic/N > 0.01$ at $\pm 0.1$~dex,
    $p_{\rm within} < 0.01$ (global shuffle),
    and best scale within $\pm 0.1$~dex of $\gdag$.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Predictions and Future Tests} \label{sec:predictions}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{BIG-SPARC} \citep{Haubner2024}: $\sim\!3{,}900$ galaxies
    with SPARC-quality 3.6\,$\mu$m mass decomposition. If the kurtosis
    spike persists at $\gdag$ with homogeneous photometry, the B-band
    artifact hypothesis is excluded. If the scatter inversion replicates,
    the PROBES failure is attributed to mass-model quality rather than
    absence of signal.
    \item \textbf{Quantum correction}: Approximately $4.75 \times 10^6$ RAR
    data points needed for a $3\sigma$ distinction between $\nbar(\nbar+1)$
    and $\nbar^2$.
    \item \textbf{High-resolution lensing}: Euclid and HSC stacked profiles
    can resolve the $2$--$10\,{\rm kpc}$ solitonic core regime currently
    unresolved in KiDS ($35\,{\rm kpc}$ inner radius).
    \item \textbf{Hydrodynamic simulation comparison}: A preliminary
    healing-length comparison using 48{,}060 TNG100 halos
    (\S\ref{sec:tng_xi}) shows that the apparent $\xi$-organizing
    signal vanishes under mass matching. The kurtosis spike and scatter
    inversion have not yet been tested in simulations with identical
    pipelines; this remains the most direct test of possibility (b).
    \item \textbf{Stellar stream gaps}: Gaia DR3 gaps probe dark
    substructure fluctuations; wave dark matter predicts super-Poissonian
    spacing.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Summary} \label{sec:summary}

The radial acceleration relation is algebraically identical to the
Bose-Einstein occupation number,
$\gdm/\gbar = 1/(e^{\sqrt{\gbar/\gdag}} - 1)$. A kernel+scale
identifiability framework with anti-numerology controls confirms that
the BE~RAR kernel is preferred over six alternatives, localizing the
preferred scale to within \ValDeltaFromGdag~dex of $\gdag$, with zero
hits in 500-realization permutation nulls and galaxy-grouped
cross-validation giving $\log_{10} s = \ValCvMean \pm \ValCvStd$.
Negative controls confirm signal collapse under perturbation; a
cluster-scale pilot correctly returns a null.

We have tested the statistical consequences of this mapping across six
datasets spanning four quality tiers and three orders of magnitude in
sample size. Three fingerprints emerge in the SPARC core sample
(131 galaxies): wave-interference variance scaling ($\daic = +10.5$),
a scatter inversion anchored at $\gdag$ to $0.05\dex$ precision, and a
candidate kurtosis spike at $\gdag$ (Tier~K only, B-band dependent).
A primary battery of 23 tests yields 10 supporting, 5 inconclusive, 1 failure
(PROBES), 4 diagnostic, and 3 in a different physical regime. Twelve
additional supplementary diagnostics are now integrated; they reinforce
non-random residual structure while explicitly exposing confounds in the
SPARC+Brouwer composite lensing bridge (Section~\ref{sec:supp12}).

The PROBES replication failure is the strongest constraint: either the
scatter inversion requires SPARC-quality mass decomposition to detect, or
it is not a universal feature of galaxy rotation curves. BIG-SPARC
($\sim\!3{,}900$ galaxies, SPARC-quality photometry) is the decisive
upcoming test.

The framework is designed to be reusable: given any $(x, y)$ dataset
and a library of candidate kernels, it returns a ranked list of kernels
with scale estimates, significance metrics, and interpretation guardrails.
We release the full framework, run folders, and reproducibility metadata
to enable independent extension to non-RAR datasets.

\section{AIC Methodology and Sensitivity} \label{app:aic}

Throughout this paper, we use the Akaike Information Criterion defined as
\begin{equation}
    {\rm AIC} = \chi^2 + 2k,
\end{equation}
where $k$ is the number of free parameters and
\begin{equation}
    \chi^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left[\frac{\log g_{{\rm obs},i}
    - \log g_{{\rm model},i}}{\sigma_i}\right]^2.
\end{equation}

For variance-scaling tests (\S\ref{sec:variance}), $\sigma_i$ is the
per-bin RMS scatter. For the Milgrom vs.\ standard RAR comparison
(\S\ref{sec:cosmo}), $\sigma_i$ is set uniformly to the global RMS under
the default weighting.

\textbf{Sensitivity of the Milgrom $\daic$.} The $\daic = -1{,}222$
preference for the Milgrom de~Sitter--Unruh form is obtained under uniform
per-point weighting in $\log\gbar$:

\label{tab:aic_sensitivity}}

\colhead{Weighting scheme} & \colhead{$\daic$}
}

Inverse-variance & $\approx -400$ \\
